# Alcohols

Alcohols are organic compounds containing one or more **hydroxyl ($-OH$)** groups attached to a saturated carbon atom. They can be thought of as derivatives of water ($H-O-H$) where one hydrogen is replaced by an alkyl group ($R-O-H$).

## 1. Classification of Alcohols
Alcohols are classified based on the environment of the carbon atom bonded to the $-OH$ group:
- **Primary ($1^\circ$):** The $-OH$ carbon is attached to only one other carbon (e.g., Ethanol, $CH_3CH_2OH$).
- **Secondary ($2^\circ$):** The $-OH$ carbon is attached to two other carbons (e.g., Isopropanol, $CH_3CH(OH)CH_3$).
- **Tertiary ($3^\circ$):** The $-OH$ carbon is attached to three other carbons (e.g., *tert*-butyl alcohol, $(CH_3)_3COH$).

## 2. Physical Properties

### A. Boiling Point (Hydrogen Bonding)
Alcohols have significantly **higher boiling points** than alkanes or ethers of similar molar mass.
- **Reason:** The highly polar $O-H$ bond allows for **intermolecular hydrogen bonding**. It takes much more energy to overcome these strong attractive forces than the weak LDFs found in hydrocarbons.
- **Trend:** BP increases with molar mass (longer chains) and decreases with branching (less surface area for LDFs).

### B. Solubility in Water
- **Small Alcohols (C1-C3):** Methanol, ethanol, and propanol are **miscible** (completely soluble) in water because they can form hydrogen bonds with water molecules.
- **Large Alcohols (C4+):** As the non-polar hydrocarbon chain (the "tail") grows longer, it overrides the polar hydroxyl group (the "head"), and solubility decreases rapidly.
- **Polyols:** Molecules with multiple $-OH$ groups (like glycerol) are highly soluble in water.

## 3. IUPAC Nomenclature Rules
Naming alcohols follows a strict hierarchy to ensure the $-OH$ group is prioritized:

1.  **Select the Parent Chain:** Find the longest continuous carbon chain that **contains the $-OH$ group**.
2.  **Number the Chain:** Number from the end **closest to the $-OH$ group**, regardless of alkyl substituents or multiple bonds. The $-OH$ carbon must have the lowest possible number.
3.  **Change the Suffix:** Drop the *-e* from the parent alkane name and add **-ol** (e.g., Ethane $\rightarrow$ Ethanol).
4.  **Indicate Position:** For chains with 3 or more carbons, use a number to show the location of the $-OH$ group (e.g., *propan-1-ol* or *1-propanol*).
5.  **Unsaturated Alcohols:** If a double or triple bond is present, the $-OH$ still takes priority for numbering. The name becomes an "en-ol" or "yn-ol" (e.g., *prop-2-en-1-ol*).
6.  **Cyclic Alcohols:** The carbon attached to the $-OH$ is **automatically Carbon 1**. You do not need a "1" in the name unless other substituents are present (e.g., *cyclopentanol*).

## 4. Synthesis of Alcohols
There are several ways to prepare alcohols in the lab and on an industrial scale:

### A. Hydration of Alkenes
The most common industrial method. Adding water to an alkene in the presence of a strong acid catalyst ($H_2SO_4$) produces an alcohol.
- **Rule:** This follows **Markovnikov's Rule** (the $-OH$ group adds to the more substituted carbon).
- $$CH_2=CH_2 + H_2O \xrightarrow{H^2SO^4} CH_3CH_2OH$$

### B. Nucleophilic Substitution
Alkyl halides react with strong bases (like $NaOH$ or $KOH$) to replace the halogen with a hydroxyl group.
- $$R-Cl + NaOH \rightarrow R-OH + NaCl$$

### C. Reduction of Carbonyl Compounds
Adding hydrogen (using reducing agents like $LiAlH_4$ or $NaBH_4$) to aldehydes and ketones:
- **Aldehydes** are reduced to **Primary Alcohols**.
- **Ketones** are reduced to **Secondary Alcohols**.

### D. Fermentation (Ethanol)
An industrial/biological process where sugars (like glucose) are broken down by yeast in the absence of oxygen.
- $$C_6H_{12}O_6 \xrightarrow{yeast} 2C_2H_5OH + 2CO_2$$

## 5. Chemical Reactivity

### A. Oxidation
The product of oxidation depends on the class of the alcohol:
- **Primary Alcohols:** Oxidize to **Aldehydes**, and then further to **Carboxylic Acids**.
- **Secondary Alcohols:** Oxidize to **Ketones**.
- **Tertiary Alcohols:** **Resistant to oxidation** because there is no hydrogen atom on the $-OH$ carbon to be removed.

### B. Dehydration (Elimination)
Heating an alcohol with a strong acid catalyst (like $H_2SO_4$ or $H_3PO_4$) removes a molecule of water to form an **alkene**.
- $$CH_3CH_2OH \xrightarrow{H_2SO_4, \Delta} CH_2=CH_2 + H_2O$$

### C. Substitution
Alcohols can react with hydrogen halides ($HX$) to form alkyl halides.
- $$R-OH + HCl \rightarrow R-Cl + H_2O$$
- *Note:* Tertiary alcohols react fastest via this pathway (Lucas Test).
