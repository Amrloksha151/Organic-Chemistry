# Aldehydes and Ketones

Aldehydes and ketones both contain the **carbonyl group ($C=O$)**, a carbon atom double-bonded to an oxygen atom. Their chemical and physical properties are largely defined by the polarity of this group.

## 1. Structural Difference
- **Aldehydes:** The carbonyl group is at the **end** of the carbon chain. The carbon is bonded to at least one hydrogen ($R-CHO$).
- **Ketones:** The carbonyl group is in the **middle** of the carbon chain. The carbon is bonded to two other carbon atoms ($R-CO-R'$).

## 2. Physical Properties

### A. Polarity and Boiling Point
The carbonyl group is highly polar because oxygen is much more electronegative than carbon ($O = 3.5, C = 2.5$).
- **Boiling Point:** They have **higher** boiling points than alkanes of similar mass due to dipole-dipole attractions. However, they have **lower** boiling points than alcohols because they **cannot form intermolecular hydrogen bonds** with themselves (they lack an $H$ bonded directly to an $O$).
- **Trend:** Alkane < Ether < **Aldehyde/Ketone** < Alcohol.

### B. Solubility
- **Small Aldehydes/Ketones (C1-C4):** Are soluble in water because the carbonyl oxygen can form hydrogen bonds with the hydrogen atoms of water molecules.
- **Large Molecules:** As the non-polar alkyl chain increases in length, water solubility decreases.

## 3. IUPAC Nomenclature

### Naming Aldehydes
1.  **Parent Chain:** Find the longest chain containing the $-CHO$ group.
2.  **Suffix:** Replace the *-e* of the alkane with **-al** (e.g., Ethane $\rightarrow$ Ethanal).
3.  **Numbering:** The carbonyl carbon is **always Carbon 1**. You do not need to include the number "1" in the name.
4.  **Example:** $CH_3CH(CH_3)CHO$ is *2-methylpropanal*.

### Naming Ketones
1.  **Parent Chain:** Find the longest chain containing the $C=O$ group.
2.  **Suffix:** Replace the *-e* of the alkane with **-one** (e.g., Propane $\rightarrow$ Propanone).
3.  **Numbering:** Number the chain from the end **closest to the carbonyl group**.
4.  **Position:** For chains with 5 or more carbons, indicate the position of the carbonyl group with a number.
5.  **Example:** $CH_3COCH_2CH_2CH_3$ is *2-pentanone*.

## 4. Synthesis via Oxidation
The most common way to synthesize aldehydes and ketones is through the controlled oxidation of [[08 - Alcohols|Alcohols]].

### A. Synthesis of Aldehydes
Oxidation of a **Primary Alcohol** ($1^\circ$) produces an aldehyde.
- **Reagent:** Pyridinium chlorochromate (PCC) is often used to stop the reaction at the aldehyde stage. Stronger oxidants like $K_2Cr_2O_7$ will oxidize it further to a carboxylic acid.
- $$R-CH_2OH \xrightarrow{[O]} R-CHO$$

### B. Synthesis of Ketones
Oxidation of a **Secondary Alcohol** ($2^\circ$) produces a ketone.
- **Reagent:** Acidified potassium dichromate ($K_2Cr_2O_7/H^+$) or potassium permanganate ($KMnO_4$). Ketones are resistant to further oxidation.
- $$R-CH(OH)-R' \xrightarrow{[O]} R-CO-R'$$

## 5. Chemical Reactivity
The primary reaction for carbonyl compounds is **Nucleophilic Addition**. Because the carbonyl carbon is electron-deficient ($\delta+$), it is susceptible to attack by nucleophiles.
- **Reduction:** Aldehydes can be reduced back to $1^\circ$ alcohols, and ketones to $2^\circ$ alcohols using $NaBH_4$ or $LiAlH_4$.
- **Addition of Grignard Reagents:** A powerful way to form new C-C bonds and create complex alcohols.
