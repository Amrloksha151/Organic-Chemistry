# Carboxylic Acids

Carboxylic acids are organic compounds characterized by the **carboxyl functional group ($-COOH$)**. This group consists of a carbonyl group ($C=O$) and a hydroxyl group ($-OH$) bonded to the same carbon atom.

## 1. Physical Properties

### A. Acidity
As the name implies, these compounds are **acidic**. They are much more acidic than alcohols because the resulting carboxylate ion ($R-COO^-$) is stabilized by **resonance**, which delocalizes the negative charge between the two oxygen atoms.
- They are weak acids compared to inorganic acids like $HCl$, but they readily react with strong bases like $NaOH$.

### B. Boiling Point and Hydrogen Bonding
Carboxylic acids have the **highest boiling points** of the functional groups discussed so far (alkanes, ethers, aldehydes, ketones, and alcohols).
- **Reason:** Each molecule can form **two hydrogen bonds** with another carboxylic acid molecule, creating a stable "dimer" structure. This effectively doubles the molecular mass that must be overcome to reach the gas phase.

### C. Solubility
- **Small Acids (C1-C4):** (Methanoic, ethanoic, propanoic, and butanoic acids) are completely miscible in water due to strong hydrogen bonding.
- **Large Acids:** Solubility decreases as the non-polar alkyl chain grows.

## 2. IUPAC Nomenclature
Naming carboxylic acids follows a high-priority set of rules:

1.  **Identify the Parent Chain:** Find the longest chain containing the $-COOH$ group.
2.  **Change the Suffix:** Replace the *-e* of the parent alkane with **-oic acid** (e.g., Methane $\rightarrow$ Methanoic acid).
3.  **Numbering:** The carboxyl carbon is **always Carbon 1**. No positional number is needed for the group itself.
4.  **Common Names:** Many are still referred to by historical common names:
    - Methanoic acid = **Formic acid** (found in ants)
    - Ethanoic acid = **Acetic acid** (vinegar)
    - Butanoic acid = **Butyric acid** (rancid butter)

## 3. Synthesis of Carboxylic Acids

### A. Oxidation of Primary Alcohols
Vigorous oxidation of a primary alcohol using a strong oxidizing agent like acidified potassium dichromate ($K_2Cr_2O_7/H^+$) or potassium permanganate ($KMnO_4$).
- $$R-CH_2OH \xrightarrow{[O]} R-CHO \xrightarrow{[O]} R-COOH$$

### B. Oxidation of Aldehydes
Aldehydes are easily oxidized to carboxylic acids using the same reagents mentioned above.
- $$R-CHO \xrightarrow{[O]} R-COOH$$

### C. Hydrolysis of Esters or Amides
Heating an ester or amide with water in the presence of an acid or base catalyst breaks the bond to yield a carboxylic acid.
- **Ester Hydrolysis:** $R-COOR' + H_2O \xrightarrow{H^+} R-COOH + R'-OH$

## 4. Chemical Reactivity

### A. Salt Formation
Reaction with bases yields a carboxylate salt and water.
- $$CH_3COOH + NaOH \rightarrow CH_3COONa + H_2O$$ (Sodium acetate)

### B. Esterification (Fischer Esterification)
Reaction with an alcohol in the presence of an acid catalyst produces an **Ester**.
- $$R-COOH + R'-OH \rightleftharpoons R-COOR' + H_2O$$

### C. Reduction
Carboxylic acids are difficult to reduce. A very strong reducing agent like **$LiAlH_4$** is required to reduce them back to primary alcohols ($NaBH_4$ is not strong enough).
