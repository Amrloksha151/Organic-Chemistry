# Esters and Other Functional Groups

This section covers the remaining important functional groups: **Esters**, **Ethers**, **Amines**, and **Amides**. These groups are essential in the study of natural products, synthetic polymers, and biochemistry.

---

## 1. Esters ($R-COOR'$)

Esters are derivatives of carboxylic acids where the $-OH$ group is replaced by an $-OR$ (alkoxy) group. They are famous for their pleasant, fruity odors.

### A. Physical Properties
- **Boiling Point:** Esters are polar but **cannot form hydrogen bonds with themselves**. Therefore, they have lower boiling points than the carboxylic acids and alcohols from which they are derived.
- **Solubility:** Small esters are somewhat soluble in water because they can accept hydrogen bonds from water molecules.
- **Odors:** Responsible for the scents of many fruits (e.g., ethyl butanoate smells like pineapple).

### B. IUPAC Nomenclature
Naming an ester is a two-part process:
1.  **First Part (Alkyl):** Identify the alkyl group attached to the oxygen atom (derived from the alcohol).
2.  **Second Part (Alkanoate):** Identify the acyl group (derived from the carboxylic acid) and change the *-ic acid* ending to **-oate**.
- **Example:** $CH_3COOCH_2CH_3$
	- Alkyl from ethanol: *Ethyl*
	- Acyl from acetic acid: *Ethanoate*
	- Name: **Ethyl ethanoate**

### C. Synthesis: Esterification
Esters are synthesized by reacting a carboxylic acid with an alcohol in the presence of an acid catalyst (usually concentrated $H_2SO_4$).
- $$R-COOH + R'-OH \rightleftharpoons R-COOR' + H_2O$$

---

## 2. Ethers ($R-O-R'$)

Ethers contain an oxygen atom bonded to two alkyl or aryl groups.

### A. Properties and Naming
- **Naming:** Identify the two alkyl groups. The smaller group is named as an **alkoxy** substituent, and the larger group is the parent alkane.
	- **Example:** $CH_3-O-CH_2CH_3$ is **Methoxyethane**.
- **Reactivity:** Ethers are relatively unreactive and are frequently used as **solvents** (e.g., Diethyl ether) because they dissolve many organic compounds without reacting with them.

---

## 3. Amines ($R-NH_2$)

Amines are derivatives of ammonia ($NH_3$) where one or more hydrogens are replaced by alkyl groups.

### A. Classification and Naming
- **Primary ($1^\circ$):** $R-NH_2$ (e.g., Methanamine).
- **Secondary ($2^\circ$):** $R_2-NH$ (e.g., N-methylmethanamine).
- **Tertiary ($3^\circ$):** $R_3-N$ (e.g., N,N-dimethylmethanamine).
- **Properties:** They are **basic** and typically have strong, "fishy" odors.

---

## 4. Amides ($R-CONH_2$)

Amides contain a carbonyl group ($C=O$) directly bonded to a nitrogen atom.

### A. Properties and Naming
- **Naming:** Identify the parent carboxylic acid and replace the *-oic acid* ending with **-amide**.
	- **Example:** $CH_3CONH_2$ is **Ethanamide**.
- **Biological Significance:** The bond between the carbonyl carbon and the nitrogen is called the **amide bond** (or **peptide bond** in proteins). It is extremely stable and forms the backbone of all proteins.
