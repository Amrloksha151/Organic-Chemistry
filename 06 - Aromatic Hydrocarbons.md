# Aromatic Hydrocarbons

Aromatic hydrocarbons, also known as **arenes**, are a class of cyclic, unsaturated compounds that possess extraordinary stability due to the delocalization of pi electrons. The simplest and most important member of this class is **Benzene ($C_6H_6$)**.

## 1. The Structure of Benzene
Benzene consists of six carbon atoms in a hexagonal ring, with one hydrogen atom attached to each carbon.
- **Hybridization:** Each carbon is **$sp^2$ hybridized**.
- **Geometry:** The molecule is perfectly flat (planar) with bond angles of $120^\circ$.
- **Bond Lengths:** All C-C bonds are of equal length ($1.39 \, \text{\AA}$), which is intermediate between a single bond ($1.54 \, \text{\AA}$) and a double bond ($1.34 \, \text{\AA}$).

### Resonance and Stability
Benzene is often drawn with alternating single and double bonds (**Kekulé structures**). However, the real molecule is a **resonance hybrid** where the six pi electrons are delocalized across the entire ring. This is often represented by a circle inside the hexagon.
- **Resonance Energy:** This delocalization makes benzene much more stable (lower in energy) than expected for a "triene."

## 2. Aromaticity (Hückel's Rule)
For a compound to be considered aromatic, it must meet four criteria:
1. **Cyclic:** The molecule must be a ring.
2. **Planar:** All atoms in the ring must lie in the same plane to allow orbital overlap.
3. **Fully Conjugated:** Every atom in the ring must have a $p$ orbital (usually $sp^2$ hybridized).
4. **Hückel's Rule:** The ring must contain **$4n + 2$** pi electrons (where $n$ is a non-negative integer: $n = 0, 1, 2, \dots$). 
   - *Example:* Benzene has 6 pi electrons ($n=1$), so it is aromatic.

## 3. Naming Aromatic Compounds
### Monosubstituted Benzenes
Many derivatives are named by adding the substituent name as a prefix to "benzene":
- **Chlorobenzene**, **Nitrobenzene**, **Ethylbenzene**.
- Some have unique **common names** used in IUPAC:
	- Methylbenzene = **Toluene**
	- Hydroxybenzene = **Phenol**
	- Aminobenzene = **Aniline**

### Disubstituted Benzenes
When two groups are present, their relative positions are indicated by numbers (1,2; 1,3; or 1,4) or by the prefixes **ortho-**, **meta-**, and **para-**:
- **ortho (o-):** 1,2-position (adjacent).
- **meta (m-):** 1,3-position (separated by one carbon).
- **para (p-):** 1,4-position (opposite).

## 4. Chemical Reactivity
Unlike alkenes, benzene **does not undergo addition reactions** under normal conditions because adding a reagent would break the stable aromatic ring. Instead, benzene undergoes **Electrophilic Aromatic Substitution (EAS)**, where a hydrogen is replaced by an electrophile.

| Reaction | Reagents | Group Introduced |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Halogenation** | $X_2, FeX_3$ | $-Cl$ or $-Br$ |
| **Nitration** | $HNO_3, H_2SO_4$ | $-NO_2$ |
| **Sulfonation** | $SO_3, H_2SO_4$ | $-SO_3H$ |
| **Friedel-Crafts Alkylation** | $R-Cl, AlCl_3$ | $-R$ (Alkyl group) |
