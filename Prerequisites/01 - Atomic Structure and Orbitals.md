To understand organic chemistry, one must first understand the behavior of electrons within an atom. Electrons are not just "dots" orbiting a nucleus; they exist in specific regions of space called **orbitals**.
<hr>
## 1. Electron Shells and Subshells
Electrons are organized into principal energy levels ($n = 1, 2, 3, \dots$). Each level contains subshells, which are further divided into orbitals.

### Subshell Types:
- **s-subshell:** 1 orbital, holds up to **2 electrons**. Spherical shape.
- **p-subshell:** 3 orbitals ($p_x, p_y, p_z$), holds up to **6 electrons**. Dumbbell shape.
- **d-subshell:** 5 orbitals, holds up to **10 electrons**. Complex shapes.
- **f-subshell:** 7 orbitals, holds up to **14 electrons**. Very complex shapes.

## 2. Electron Configuration Rules
How do we know where the electrons go? We follow three primary rules:

### A. The Aufbau Principle
Electrons fill the lowest energy orbitals first. 
*Order:* $1s < 2s < 2p < 3s < 3p < 4s < 3d \dots$

### B. The Pauli Exclusion Principle
An orbital can hold a maximum of **two electrons**, and they must have **opposite spins** (represented as $\uparrow\downarrow$).

### C. Hund's Rule
When filling orbitals of equal energy (like the three $p$ orbitals), electrons will occupy empty orbitals singly before pairing up. This minimizes electron-electron repulsion.

## 3. The Carbon Atom
In [[01 - Basics and Definitions|Organic Chemistry]], carbon is the star. Let's look at its ground-state configuration:
- **Atomic Number:** 6
- **Configuration:** $1s^2 2s^2 2p^2$
- **Valence Electrons:** 4 (the electrons in the outermost $n=2$ shell).

These 4 valence electrons are what allow carbon to form four bonds, but to do so efficiently, it often undergoes **hybridization**, which you can read about in [[01 - Basics and Definitions#Orbital Hybridization]].

## 4. Why Orbitals Matter in Organic Chemistry
The overlap of these orbitals creates chemical bonds:
- **Sigma ($\sigma$) bonds:** Head-on overlap of orbitals.
- **Pi ($\pi$) bonds:** Side-on overlap of $p$ orbitals.

Understanding the "shape" of these orbitals helps us predict the 3D structure of molecules, which is covered in [[03 - VSEPR Theory and Molecular Geometry]].
