Lewis structures (also known as electron dot structures) are simplified representations of the valence electrons in a molecule. They help us visualize how atoms are bonded and where lone pairs of electrons reside.
<hr>
## 1. The Octet Rule
Most atoms are stable when they have **8 electrons** in their valence shell (matching the configuration of a noble gas).
- **Exceptions:** 
	- **Hydrogen:** Stable with only 2 electrons (a "duet").
	- **Boron/Beryllium:** Often electron-deficient (6 or 4 electrons).
	- **Expanded Octets:** Elements in Period 3 and below (like P, S) can hold more than 8 electrons due to available $d$ orbitals.

## 2. Steps to Drawing Lewis Structures

### Step 1: Count Total Valence Electrons
Sum the valence electrons for all atoms in the molecule.
- If it's a **cation** (positive charge), subtract electrons.
- If it's an **anion** (negative charge), add electrons.

### Step 2: Determine the Central Atom
The least electronegative atom is usually the central atom (Carbon is almost always central; Hydrogen is NEVER central).

### Step 3: Draw Single Bonds
Connect the outer atoms to the central atom using single lines (each line represents 2 electrons).

### Step 4: Distribute Remaining Electrons
1. Fill the octets of the **outer atoms** first.
2. If electrons remain, place them on the **central atom**.

### Step 5: Form Multiple Bonds (If Necessary)
If the central atom does not have an octet, move lone pairs from outer atoms to form double or triple bonds with the central atom.

## 3. Formal Charge
Formal charge helps us determine the most plausible Lewis structure when multiple options exist.
$$\text{Formal Charge} = (\text{Valence Electrons}) - (\text{Non-bonding Electrons}) - \frac{1}{2}(\text{Bonding Electrons})$$
*Rule:* The best structure is the one where formal charges are closest to zero.

## 4. Resonance
Sometimes, a single Lewis structure cannot accurately describe a molecule (e.g., Benzene or Carbonate $CO_3^{2-}$). In these cases, we draw multiple **resonance structures** connected by double-headed arrows ($\leftrightarrow$). The actual molecule is a "hybrid" of these structures.
- Resonance is a key concept in [[05 - Isomerism]] and stability in organic reactions.
